Executive Summary

The 2026 Iran conflict represents the most significant geopolitical shock to global energy markets since the 1973 OPEC embargo. Unlike the 2003 Iraq invasion—which was rapid and relatively contained—this conflict combines Strait of Hormuz closure risk, IAEA inspection expulsion, Gulf Coalition military mobilization, and credible Iranian ballistic and drone capacity that threatens critical infrastructure across the Arabian Peninsula and beyond.

Our Baseline Scenario projects a 24-month active conflict phase, Brent crude peaking at $148/bbl within months 3–6, global inflation adding 3.8 percentage points in Year 1, and a cumulative global GDP loss of approximately $4.2 trillion over three years. The Middle East, East Africa, and South Asia bear the greatest humanitarian costs. North America and Australia, as net energy exporters, experience relative economic advantage offset by financial market contagion.

The Pessimistic Scenario (P=28%) involves Iranian closure of the Strait of Hormuz, triggering oil at $195–220/bbl, global recession in Years 1–2, and a potential fracture of the petrodollar system as China and Russia deepen alternative payment rails. The Optimistic Scenario (P=22%) assumes a ceasefire by Month 10, oil stabilizing at $105, and rapid reconstruction demand driving MENA equities higher.

Investor Action: Overweight defense (Raytheon, BAE Systems, Elbit), energy producers (Saudi Aramco, ExxonMobil, Woodside), and reconstruction infrastructure (Fluor, Bechtel-linked ETFs). Underweight aviation, luxury consumer, and Emerging Market sovereigns with high energy import dependency. Hedge with gold, USD, and GSCI commodity futures.

Key Assumptions & Methodology

Scenario Framework

  • Baseline (P=50%): 24-month conflict, partial Hormuz disruption
  • Optimistic (P=22%): Ceasefire Month 10, Hormuz reopens Month 8
  • Pessimistic (P=28%): Full Hormuz closure 6+ months, regional expansion
  • Black swan tail: Nuclear threshold breach (P=4%) not modeled

Historical Precedents

  • 1973 Oil Embargo: CPI +9%, GDP −2.9% OECD (18-month lag)
  • 1990–91 Gulf War: Oil spike +140%, receded in 6 months
  • 2003 Iraq: Minimal oil disruption; different dynamics
  • 2022 Russia-Ukraine: Energy price +200%, Europe CPI +10%
  • Hormuz closure analog: 1987–88 Tanker War partial disruption

Energy Assumptions

  • Iran produces ~3.3M bbl/day (pre-conflict)
  • Hormuz: 20% of global oil, 18% of LNG transits daily
  • SPR releases (US, IEA) offset ~8–12 weeks of supply gap
  • Saudi spare capacity: ~2.5M bbl/day (short-term relief)
  • Shale ramp: 6–9 month lag to meaningful volume increase

Macro Model Inputs

  • Inflation transmission: +$10/bbl oil = +0.3% CPI (import-heavy economies)
  • Trade multiplier: Shipping +100% = −0.4% global trade volume
  • Confidence shock: Regional war premium = −0.5 to −1.2% GDP
  • Defense spend crowding: +1% GDP defense = −0.2% private invest.
  • Currency: Flight-to-USD = −10–20% EM FX basket

Key Uncertainties

  • Iranian ballistic missile accuracy vs. Saudi oil infrastructure
  • Chinese policy: Will Beijing sanction-bust or comply?
  • US domestic political appetite for sustained engagement
  • Alternative energy substitution speed (LNG, renewables)
  • Global recession contagion via credit market tightening

  • Abqaiq/Ras Tanura precision strike (2019 analog, 10× scale)
  • Removes 8–10M bbl/day for 3–12 months of repair
